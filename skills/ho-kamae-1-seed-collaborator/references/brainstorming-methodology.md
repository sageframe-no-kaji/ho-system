# On Brainstorming

## A methodology for generating ideas that matter

---

Most people think brainstorming is what happens when you sit around a table and say things. It isn't. What happens around most tables is a performance of ideation — people offering one idea each, editing themselves in real time, converging on the first thing that sounds reasonable, and calling it a plan. That isn't brainstorming. That's a meeting.

Brainstorming — real brainstorming, the kind that produces ideas worth building — is a structured practice with distinct phases, specific rules, and a discipline that can be taught. I have been teaching it for fifteen years, across contexts ranging from sixteen-year-olds in a design studio to corporate teams to educators learning to facilitate creative work in their classrooms. The practice I teach draws from architecture studio pedagogy, from the iterative design tradition, and from a simple observation: people do not naturally generate good ideas. They generate comfortable ideas. The distance between comfortable and good is the entire practice.

---

## Where This Comes From

In architecture school, you do not brainstorm by talking. You brainstorm by drawing. You brainstorm by building. You brainstorm by studying what already exists — not to copy it, but to understand the territory well enough that your own ideas have ground underneath them. The studio process is a cycle: research, ideate, make, critique, revise. It repeats until the work is good, and "good" is defined by a community of people who will tell you honestly when it isn't.

I built on this tradition at NuVu Studio, an innovation school in Cambridge, where as Chief Academic Officer I codified the curriculum, pedagogy, and training systems that supported educators in facilitating this work. Students as young as fourteen worked through two-week design intensives producing real projects — not exercises, not simulations, but functioning prototypes that addressed genuine problems. I refined it further at ARCK, training educators to facilitate this kind of work in their own classrooms. I adapted it at Citizen Schools, integrating it into an experience-based learning framework that reached thousands of students through community mentors.

Across all of these contexts, the core process stayed the same. The vocabulary shifted. The materials changed. The audience ranged from teenagers to professionals. But the methodology — the actual structure of how ideas are generated, evaluated, and developed — was consistent. What I'm articulating here is that methodology.

## The Problem With Ideas

People have a romantic notion of ideas: that they arrive suddenly, fully formed, from some mysterious source. The shower insight. The napkin sketch. The eureka moment.

This is mostly nonsense. What people experience as sudden insight is almost always the visible tip of a process that happened below the surface — days or weeks of engagement with a problem, conscious or not, that finally coalesced into something they could articulate. The idea didn't come from nowhere. It came from the accumulated friction of living with a problem.

The real issue is not how to have an idea. It is how to have _enough_ ideas — and how to have them without immediately killing the ones that seem strange, impractical, or uncomfortable. Self-editing is the enemy of brainstorming. It operates in real time, silently, and it is devastatingly effective. The voice that says "that's stupid" or "that won't work" or "people will think I'm ridiculous" kills ideas before they can be examined, combined, or developed into something meaningful.

Every technique I teach is, at some level, a strategy for defeating self-editing long enough to let ideas exist.

## The Structure

Good brainstorming has phases. They are not optional, and they are not interchangeable. The discipline is in knowing which phase you are in and behaving accordingly.

### Phase 1: Framing

Before anyone generates a single idea, the problem space must be defined. Not solved — defined. What is the territory? What are the constraints? What has been tried before? What are the precedents — not as inspiration, but as structural understanding of the landscape?

Framing is where precedential thinking lives. You study what exists: conceptual precedents that explore the ideas at play, visionary precedents that show what's possible at the edge of the field, and comparable precedents that demonstrate what's achievable at the scale you're working at. Through a critical melding of these categories, you develop an understanding of the territory that is expansive, not narrow. Not "I liked this one project" but "here is how this problem has been approached across disciplines, across cultures, across time — and here is where the genuine gap lives."

Framing also includes the Drill Down — a technique I adapted from Priya Parker's work on gathering design. You take the surface-level statement of what you want to do and keep asking why until you hit a belief or value. "I want to build a tool for X" becomes "because the current options fail at Y" becomes "because the people who have this problem experience Z" becomes "because I believe that..." — and that belief is the real foundation. Without it, you're building on sand.

The output of framing is not an idea. It is a rich, researched, personally grounded understanding of the problem space from which ideas can emerge.

### Phase 2: Generative Explosion

This is where most people's notion of brainstorming lives — and where most people do it wrong.

The goal of this phase is volume. Not quality. Volume. The mundane to the preposterous. Wild ideas, obvious ideas, impossible ideas, stupid ideas, ideas that contradict each other. The rules are explicit:

**Encourage wild ideas.** Wild ideas give rise to creative leaps. The preposterous idea that nobody takes seriously often contains a kernel that, when extracted and developed, becomes the most original solution in the room.

**Suspend judgment.** You do not evaluate during this phase. Not your own ideas, not anyone else's. The evaluative voice is useful — it just isn't useful yet. It gets its turn in Phase 3.

**Go for quantity.** More ideas means more options, more combinations, more chances for something surprising to emerge. I teach a technique I call the Power of 3: for every framing of the problem, generate at least three approaches. For every approach, generate at least three ideas. The structure defeats self-editing by making volume a structural requirement, not a suggestion. Three approaches times three ideas is nine. Three framings times three approaches times three ideas is twenty-seven. Most of them will be bad. That's the point. The five good ones wouldn't have emerged without the twenty-two bad ones creating the space.

**Build on ideas.** Don't just generate in parallel — combine, extend, mutate. "What if we took that idea and combined it with this constraint?" is a brainstorming move. Additive, not competitive.

**Be concrete.** In studio, this means sketch it. In a text-based environment, it means describe it specifically enough that someone else could evaluate it. "Something with data" is not an idea. "A dashboard that shows your family members' locations and changes color when everyone is home" is an idea. Specificity is what makes ideas evaluable.

This phase should feel slightly uncomfortable. If everyone in the room (or the conversation) is nodding along comfortably, you're not brainstorming — you're confirming. The discomfort of wild, unedited, possibly-bad ideas is the signal that the process is working.

### Phase 3: Evaluative Discussion

Now the critical voice gets its turn.

The goal of this phase is to draw out, classify, and evaluate the volume produced in Phase 2. Not to pick one — to understand what you have. Which ideas have the most potential? Which ones have a specific audience? Which ones connect to the precedential research from Phase 1? Which ones are actually three different ideas wearing one label?

This is where the facilitator — whether human or AI — shifts posture. In Phase 2, you were a co-brainstormer: encouraging, building, adding. In Phase 3, you are a critic: naming strengths, identifying weaknesses, subtly elevating the ideas with the most ground under them, pushing back on the ones that sound exciting but have no foundation.

The critical move is to do this through questions, not declarations. "Who actually has this problem?" is a question. "That won't work" is a shutdown. The evaluative phase should surface the criteria — the standards against which ideas are being measured — and let those criteria do the work.

One of the most important things to catch in this phase: the same idea in different clothes. A student once showed me three "different" prototypes that were all the same concept in different sizes and materials. She thought she had three ideas. She had one idea and two variations. Real divergence means genuinely different approaches — different framings of the problem, different audiences, different mechanisms. If all three ideas assume the same architecture, you haven't brainstormed. You've decorated.

### Phase 4: Focused Development

Once a direction is chosen — not finalized, chosen — the process recurses. You take the chosen direction and generate multiple manifestations of it, each supported by precedents. Three sketches. Three approaches to the same concept. Each one explored enough that you can evaluate it against the core idea.

This is the Power of 3 applied recursively: concept → three approaches → choose one → three manifestations → choose one → three refinements. At each level, the divergence defeats premature convergence. You are always generating multiple options before committing to one.

The expectation — stated explicitly and repeatedly — is that the final project will differ from the original idea. This is not failure. This is the process working. The distance between the first idea and the final realization is the evidence that thinking happened. If the final project looks exactly like the first brainstorm, either the first idea was genuinely perfect (rare) or the process was cosmetic (common).

## The Thesis Test

You know an idea is ready to leave the brainstorming phase when the person can state it in two sentences:

**The soul:** What is the idea of this project in conceptual terms? One sentence that paints a picture in the reader's mind. This is the _why_ — the meaning, the intent, the thing that makes this project matter.

**The body:** What is the basic technical or physical construction of the project? One sentence that describes what it actually is, to someone with no technical knowledge. This is the _what_ — the form, the mechanism, the thing you're building.

If the person can state both clearly, they have an idea. If they can state the body but not the soul, they have a solution without a problem. If they can state the soul but not the body, they have a philosophy without a project. Both gaps are worth naming because both are addressable — they're not failures, they're information about where the thinking needs to go next.

## On Teaching This

The hardest thing to teach is not the technique. It is the posture.

People — especially adults, especially professionals — have spent years developing their evaluative voice. It is strong, fast, and constantly active. Asking them to suspend it, even temporarily, feels wrong. It feels like lowering their standards. It feels like being asked to generate bad ideas on purpose.

Which is exactly what it is. The willingness to generate bad ideas is the prerequisite for generating good ones. The evaluative voice doesn't go away — it gets deployed at the right time, which is not the beginning.

The facilitator's role evolves through the phases. In the generative phase, you are a collaborator — you brainstorm alongside the person, you build on their ideas, you contribute your own, you model the suspension of judgment. You say "yes, and" — not as a technique, but because you genuinely want to see where the idea goes before you evaluate it.

In the evaluative phase, you shift to provocateur. You ask the hard questions. You name the weakness. You push on whether the idea serves the problem or just sounds interesting. You connect back to the framing: does this address what we learned in the precedential research? Does this serve the audience we defined? Is this actually different from what already exists, or have we reinvented the wheel with better branding?

The transition between these postures is itself something you teach by modeling. Students — and adults — learn when to generate and when to evaluate by watching you do it. If you critique during the generative phase, they learn that generating is risky. If you generate during the evaluative phase, they learn that evaluation is optional. The phases have to be real for the facilitator, not just for the participant.

A few specific principles I've learned from teaching this across fifteen years and very different populations:

**Three prototypes, not one.** Always require at least three genuinely different approaches before allowing anyone to commit. Not three versions of the same thing — three different framings, three different mechanisms, three different ways the project could work. This is the single most effective structural rule I've found for preventing premature convergence.

**Sketching is not art.** One of the biggest barriers to brainstorming is the fear that an idea has to look good before it can be shared. Sketching is thinking on paper — it's a tool for developing and communicating meaning, not a demonstration of artistic ability. I say this explicitly and repeatedly, and I model it by sketching badly and often during critiques.

**Better to develop an amazing incomplete idea than to complete a mediocre one.** Especially in the early and middle stages. The pressure to finish pushes people toward safe, achievable, boring ideas. The discipline of staying with a strong-but-unfinished idea long enough to develop it fully is one of the most valuable things the iterative process teaches.

**When someone is stuck, change the medium.** Stop talking and draw. Stop drawing and build. Stop building and research. Stop researching and talk to someone. The block is often not a thinking problem but a medium problem — the ideas are there, but the current form of expression isn't letting them out.

**The Drill Down is the most powerful single technique.** When someone comes in with "I want to build X," the first move is always: why? And then: why is that important? And then: what would be different if this existed? Keep going until you hit something that matters — a belief, a value, a personal experience. That's the foundation. Everything else is architecture on top of it.

## What This Is Not

This is not design thinking. I have complicated feelings about design thinking — it took some genuinely useful practices from studio pedagogy and flattened them into a five-step process that could be put on a poster. Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. The stages are correct. The implication that they are linear, neat, and completable in a workshop is not.

What I teach is messier, more rigorous, and more honest about how ideas actually develop. The path from idea to realization is not a straight line. It is not even the familiar squiggly line that people put in their presentations about design process. It is a series of choice points — moments where you evaluate where you are, decide where to go, and commit to a direction knowing you might reverse it. The squiggly line implies chaos. Choice points imply agency. The difference matters.

This is also not "just brainstorming." Brainstorming as most people practice it — unstructured, unevaluated, documented on sticky notes that go in the trash — is a social ritual, not a creative practice. What I'm describing is a disciplined, phase-specific, iterative process that connects ideation to research, research to precedent, precedent to context, and context to a specific, defensible, personally grounded idea that is ready to be built.
